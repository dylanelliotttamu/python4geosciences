1. Introduction¶

Its widely recognised that carbon dioxide emissions are the primary driver of global climate change. To avoid the worst impacts of climate change, the world needs to urgently reduce emissions. At the 2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference near Paris, France, an international treaty on climate change was negotiated and passed by 196 parties. The treaty, called the Paris Agreement, covers climate change mitigation, adaptation, and finance.

Its goal is to limit global warming to well below 2, preferably to 1.5 degrees Celsius, compared to pre-industrial levels. To achieve this long-term temperature goal, countries aim to reach global peaking of greenhouse gas emissions as soon as possible to achieve a climate neutral world by mid-century.

Now, six years has passed and I'd like to examine if the agreement has made a difference or not.

2. Method¶

The realize our goal, the key is to make a precise projection for the greenhouse gas emissions since 2015 in a world without the Paris Agreement. Fossil CO$_2$ is the most important pathway among various greenhouse gases emissions. In addition, carbon emssions should be closed linked with the atmospheric CO$_2$ concentration. According to the results of homework in the sixth week, the third order polynomial method fits the available observational CO$_2$ records and seems to provide a reasonable prediction for the future. Therefore, I decide to apply the third order polynomial fit to the fossil carbon emission datasets before 2015 and calculate the corresponding projection for recent six years. A comparison with the real emission values will tell us if the Paris Agreement has made a difference or not.

3. Datasets¶

The estimates of global and national fossil are based on the the Global Carbon Project (GCP). Their dataset covers a period from 1750 to present and even subdivides emissions according to the sources (e.g., coal, oil, gas and so on). The buildup of the datasets starts from 2001, guaranteeing the data quality. The details it covered and the quality make this a perfect dataset to help realize my object.
To visulize the global data, the country geometry of 110 meters resolution is used here. It's provided as class materials and the source is unclear.

def fit_measure_comparison(dataframe, label_name):
    # input: 
    # dataframe: a dataset include the fossil emissions and the corresponding years, pandas dataframe, default time-indexed
    # label_name: the label for the dataset, Global or country name
    # function: calculate the difference between the projection and measured results.
    # output: a figure, projection values, measured values, difference
    fit = np.polyfit(dataframe.loc[1950:2015].index, dataframe.loc[1950:2015].Total, 3)
    timefit = np.arange(1950,2021)
    emission_fit = np.polyval(fit,timefit)
    
    # plot the figure

    fig = plt.figure(figsize=(10, 6))  
    ax = fig.add_subplot(1,1,1)  
    ax.plot(dataframe.loc[1950:].index,dataframe.loc[1950:].Total,lw = 2, color = "red", alpha = 1, label = "measured data")
    ax.plot(timefit, emission_fit, '--', lw = 2, color = 'orange', alpha = 1, label = '3rd order polynomial fit')
    ax.set_xlabel('Time (year)',fontsize=16)
    ax.set_ylabel('Fossil CO$_2$ emissions (million tons)',fontsize=16)
    ax.set_title(f'Measured data vs fitting results ({label_name})',fontsize = 20)
    
    # spines
    ax.spines['right'].set_visible(False)
    ax.spines['top'].set_visible(False)
    
    # legend
    ax.legend(fontsize = 'x-large', edgecolor = "white")
    
    plt.xticks(fontsize=12)
    plt.yticks(fontsize=12)
    
    measure_integral = dataframe.loc[2016:].Total.sum()
    projection_integral = np.sum(emission_fit[-5:])
    print(f"Projected {label_name} emissions from 2016 to 2020: ", f'{projection_integral:.0f}', " million tons")
    print(f"Measured {label_name} emissions froom 2016 to 2020: ", f'{measure_integral:.0f}', " million tons")
    delta = measure_integral-projection_integral
    
    return measure_integral, projection_integral, delta

5. Conclusions¶

  1. The Paris Agreement have made a difference, slowing down the growth of global fossil carbon emissions.
  2. Except Russia, the top five major carbon contributors all have a lower emission value after 2015 than their previous trend suggests.
  3. Our results are highly sensitive to the fitting method used for projection. Nevertheless, the function provided here will make it convenient for users to try other fitting methods.
